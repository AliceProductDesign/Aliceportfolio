The Challenge

Design a booking experience that earns trust before asking for anything.

More than 40 million Americans are uninsured, and many delay care because they can't predict what it will cost.

Healthcare apps designed to help them consistently fail at the first moment of contact by hiding pricing behind sign-up walls, using opaque fee structures, and triggering the exact distrust these users already carry toward the healthcare system.

The Solution

A mobile-first healthcare booking platform for uninsured and self-pay patients, built entirely around one promise: show users exactly what they will pay before they commit.

Delayed authentication until after users have seen pricing and availability.

Flat-fee pricing on provider cards, billing accuracy sub-ratings, "What's Not Included" disclosures, and persistent No Surprise Bill messaging throughout the flow. Validated through moderated usability testing.

The Problem

The cost of care is a barrier before a single appointment is booked.

Uninsured Americans (roughly 26 million people) face a healthcare system designed around insurance. Searching for a provider means navigating a landscape of hidden pricing, credential ambiguity, and opaque booking flows.

RapiMD's core hypothesis: if you show uninsured patients the exact cost of a visit before they commit, and give them enough information about a provider to feel confident booking, you remove the primary barrier to care.

The design challenge was making that transparency feel trustworthy, not transactional.

The three-sided problem

Patient Access Gap

Background

Many uninsured or high-deductible patients delay or avoid medical care due to high upfront costs and lack of transparent pricing.

Problem

Cost-sensitive patients need an affordable, transparent way to find and book care without gambling on the final price.

Business Impact

This results in lost patient acquisition opportunities and prevents providers from filling unused appointment capacity with a new customer segment.

Provider Revenue Loss

Background

Medical practices frequently experience last-minute cancellations and unfilled appointment slots.

Problem

Practices lack a dynamic system to redistribute these time-sensitive openings to patients willing to book at a lower cost.

Business Impact

Empty slots reduce operational efficiency, lower daily revenue, and increase financial strain on small practices.

Trust & Booking Friction

Background

Patients seeking discounted care often question legitimacy, quality, and transparency when pricing deviates from standard rates.

Problem

Patients need reassurance that discounted care is still credible — there's currently no standardized, accessible way to communicate provider credibility, pricing transparency, and booking certainty.

Business Impact

Without trust signals and clarity, users abandon bookings, reducing conversion rates and limiting platform growth.

Delayed Authentication

The most important product decision.

Standard healthcare apps put account creation before value. For uninsured patients already primed to distrust systems that take without giving, this is a trust-ending moment.

RapiMD flipped the sequence entirely. Authentication was delayed until after users had selected a provider and an appointment slot — after they'd seen the flat fee, confirmed availability, and decided they wanted to proceed.

[Slot-hold countdown screen — "Account needed to continue," 06:12 timer]
[Account creation screen — Google/Apple/email, "Continue as guest"]

To prevent slot abandonment, the app reassured users their selected appointment was being held while a streamlined account creation flow was required to confirm and lock in the booking.

Logo & Brand Identity Rationale

The rapiMD logo was designed to communicate the platform's core promise.

Fast, confirmed, affordable care — in a single mark.

The logo uses a checkmark over the letter "D," signaling speed and medical completion: in healthcare, a checkmark means done, confirmed, safe. The lowercase "rapi" keeps the wordmark approachable and consumer-friendly, while the uppercase "MD" anchors it in clinical credibility — the same tension the product itself navigates.

Deep navy and red were chosen deliberately: navy builds institutional trust, while red appears exclusively on the checkmark accent and key CTAs, training the eye to pay attention when it matters.

The Gabarito and Inconsolata type pairing reinforces the transparency promise at a subconscious level — Inconsolata's monospace precision feels exact and receipt-like, aligned with a product whose entire value proposition is showing users exactly what they will pay.

Trust Architecture

Every screen was designed to build or maintain trust.

Trust architecture in healthcare UX means designing information hierarchy, interaction patterns, and visual language specifically to reduce patient anxiety and build confidence at each decision point. This included: transparent flat fees visible before login, provider credential display with verification signals, clear scope-of-service boundaries, and intentional friction at irreversible actions.

Usability Testing

Testing focus: pricing comprehension, booking confidence, trust perception, data transparency. Two failures emerged.

5Participants
9Screens tested
100%Task Completion Rate
45–60Minutes per session

Participants were interpreting itemized breakdowns as fees stacked on top of the flat fee — the exact opposite of what the design intended. Fix: a dominant "Your total: $85 flat" summary line above the breakdown. In follow-up testing, the misreading disappeared.

Participants felt uneasy when location-based results appeared without explicit permission context. Fix: explicit location permission prompt before any location-based results, with clear language about data use.

What This Project Taught Me About Trust

Trust is not a visual property.

You cannot design trust with just a color palette or reassuring headlines. Trust is built through clear expectations, explicit consent, transparent system behavior, and predictable outcomes — through what the product does, not how it looks.

For uninsured patients who have been systemically failed by healthcare, uncertainty itself is a barrier to care. Every interaction pattern that introduced ambiguity was a product failure with real consequences for real people.

Reflections

What I'd Do Differently

With more time, I would run a second testing round specifically on the revised pricing hierarchy to confirm the fix held across different literacy levels. I'd also have pushed the billing accuracy rating system further — it's the feature most likely to drive long-term platform trust and deserved more design depth in V1.
